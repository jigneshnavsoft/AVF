    Research: Accessibility

    What are some of the ways that you could change your HTML on your Demo app to be more accessible? (Remember, you can use the Section tag with jQuery, for instance)

    • - Audio captcha for all my web forms.
    • - Meaningful labels for every single form element.
    • - Meaningful labels with alternative text description for every single graphic.
    • - Never make it a requirement for the end user to put their mouse pointer on a map graphic, before they could use my demo app.
    • - Audio Responses and Input as an option for visually & mobility impaired end users.
    • - Responses and content as a PDF alternative because it's a common practice for visual & mobile impaired to use their screen readers to have PDF files read out loud to them.

    Visit a video service on the web (like YouTube) and search for "Screen Reader Demo" and "Mobile Screen Reader" - what are some of the common frustrations (or common support mechanisms) that you see among the examples that are given - how could you fix them (or use them)?

    • Cluttered websites full of advertisements are useless for visual & mobile impaired end users.

    • - Remove clutter.

      - Practice sound Usability Design guidelines.

      - Get rid of the advertisements or at least move the advertisements to a meaningful labeled section of the web page.

      - HotKeys for headings and form controls.

      - HotKeys that follow the HotKey practices of the most popular websites will be useful because visual & mobile impaired end users do not have to dedicate more time to re-learning a new way to navigate. The most popular websites have already established Hotkey navigation that visual & mobile impaired end users have become accustomed to.

    • Websites that lack meaningful labels on the graphics are non-accessible.

    • Include meaningful labels and alternative text for graphics.

    • Requiring end users to put their mouse pointer on a small graphic of a map is impossible for those that are visual or mobile impaired.

    • Speech input options for visual or mobile impaired end users to be able to speak their input. Many computers and mobile devices have this feature already available and a developer can tap into those speech recognition features from their own websites and software.

    • Captcha forms make it impossible for visual impaired end users to submit a form.

    • - Audio captcha will make it possible for those end users to submit the form while still providing the extra security of blocking out spam bots.

      - Mobile magnifcation and zoom can help visual & mobile impaired users navigate websites and mobile apps easier.

      - Help Wizards & Getting Started Wizards that work with the screen reader features can help make it easier and more effective for visual & mobile impaired users to navigate and access exactly what they are looking for on a website and within a mobile app.

      - Provide exact explicit content headers, navigation menu items that use common grammar so that visually impaired can find what they are looking for while they 'guess' what options might be available on your website or within your app.

      - Sidewalk Navigation to provide location details that are helpful for those that rely on public transportation.

      - Do not make touch tone a requirement for navigating mobile apps and phone help systems.

      - Label every single form control.

      - Offer large font-size and print options that are easy to access with common HotKeys.

    Devices: Please list two devices (and some of their features) that are made specifically to support Accessibility.

    • - MobilePal + GPS was actually created to provide security for travelers. It's a phone with only a big red call button and a small alarm button. The traveler presses the red button and a concierge responds to them and provides directions, calls the travelers friends & family, and can send emergency responders ( police, ambulance ) to the travelers location. The MobilePal sends the travelers location to the concierge automatically. It's being improved so that visually impaired people will be able to use it for sidewalk travel directions and public transportation details that are a necessity for visual & mobile impaired travelers.
    • - BrailleNote mPower converts braille documents to and from microsoft office documents to make it easier for visually impaired to share information with those that are not visually impaired. A querty or braille keyboard is included.

    Mobile Apps: Please list two apps (either Android or iOS, or both) that are made specifically to support Accessibility.

    • - iOS; provides VoiceOver installed by default in the iPhone and iPad. While it's not a seperate app that can be found in the app store, it's worth mentioning because as a Mobile Developer I can build my apps to be able to work with the VoiceOver features.
    • - iOS: LookTel Money Reader app claims to instantly recognize currency and will say the denomination out loud so that visual impaired and blind people can count and identify their currency. It uses the Mac's camera and can recognize US bills, Euro, British Pound, Australian bills, and Canadian bills.
    • - iOS: Braille Visual Translator for iPhone translates the Italian Braille Table and computer Braille characters.
    • - Android: Mobile Accessibility. This is a screen-access app made for blind people. It includes a screen reader and 10 applications for blind people, including email, gps, and phone.
    • - Android: Talkback, Kickback, Soundback. These are system applications that add audio, vibration, and spoken features to the android device and they can be turned on and off in the system settings.
